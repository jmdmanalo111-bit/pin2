Foundation & Methodology

Core principles behind Eric Jordan’s 25 Factors & 5 Senses methodology.

How much is my business worth in Canada?

Eric Jordan’s proprietary 25 Factors Affecting Business Valuation and 5 Senses Inspection Reports reflect Canadian law and fair market value. Supported by AI research and verified by lawyers, the valuation covers both tangible and intangible assets and is practical, credible, and hard to dispute.

What valuation method is best for my company?

The 25 Factors method plus the 5 Senses Inspection Report captures real value beyond formulas, including intangibles like trust, management, and market presence.

How can I increase my business’s value before selling?

Identify weak spots and strengthen intangibles (loyalty, management capability, systems). The 5 Senses approach creates a practical roadmap to maximize pre-sale value.

What factors most influence business value?

Strong, consistent net income; management strength; customer loyalty; scalability; and other intangibles weighed within the 25-Factor framework.

Valuation Process & Preparation

What’s involved, timing, cost, and how to prepare.

What is the process and how long does a valuation take?

Typical turnaround is 10 days to 2 weeks. After collecting financials and key background information, analysis of tangible and intangible assets is performed using the 25 Factors + 5 Senses methodology.

How much does a business valuation cost?

Fees generally range from $1,500–$15,000 depending on complexity; many complete valuations average about $3,500.

What documents or information do I need to provide?

Usually 3–5 years of financials and tax returns, ownership structure, customer/supplier details, and key agreements (leases, contracts, etc.).

How do I prepare my financials for a valuation or sale?

Update and normalize financials; adjust owner compensation to market; separate personal from business expenses; document non-recurring items to derive Normalized Net Income.

How are owner’s salary and expenses adjusted?

Normalization sets owner salary to fair market and adds back discretionary/personal expenses to reveal true profitability.

Timing and Frequency

When to get valued—and how often.

When is the best time to have my business valued?

Not just before selling—valuations guide growth, financing, planning, and course corrections at any stage.

How often should my business be valued?

Every 2–3 years, or with major changes (new partner, sale prep, restructuring, tax planning).

Legal and Regulatory Compliance

CRA readiness, fair market value, and who sets the rules.

Will the valuation be defendable to CRA or in court?

The framework is designed to be fact-based, transparent, and aligned with Canadian fair market value concepts used in practice.

How is fair market value determined?

Common Canadian practice looks to a notional price between informed, prudent parties acting at arm’s length with no compulsion.

How does Canadian tax law affect valuation?

Transactions like reorganizations or estate planning often require careful valuation work aligned to applicable tax requirements.

Are Canadian standards different from the US or global norms?

Approaches can differ by jurisdiction; this framework centers on Canadian practice while adapting to broader contexts.

Who regulates professional valuators in Canada?

Professional designations and practices are overseen by relevant Canadian bodies; tax matters reference the Income Tax Act and administrative practice.

Do I need a CBV, or can my accountant do it?

What matters most is that the work is competent and suitable for its purpose; some use CBVs, others use experienced practitioners familiar with the intended use.

Valuation Details and Industry Context

Multiples, projections, comparables, and operational factors.

Which multiples are typical in my industry?

Multiples vary by sector, size, growth, and risk. Normalized earnings and qualitative factors help select defensible ranges.

Are forecasts and profit projections used?

Yes—when supportable. Evidence-based assumptions help tie projections to reality.

How do you benchmark against comparable sales?

Private comparables can be opaque; triangulate with normalized financials and qualitative risk factors rather than over-relying on thin data.

How do customer base, location, and management affect worth?

Diversified customers, strong location, and capable management tend to lower risk and support higher value.

Negotiation & Risk Assessment

Resolving price gaps and surfacing hidden risks.

What if buyer and seller can’t agree on price?

A structured, evidence-based valuation creates a shared factual baseline. Tools like earn-outs can bridge expectation gaps.

What hidden risks or liabilities affect value?

Owner dependence, customer concentration, legal issues, and outdated assets are common; systematically identify and address them.

What are common errors in valuation?

Overusing rules of thumb, skipping normalization, aggressive growth assumptions, and ignoring intangibles.

Can multiple valuation methods be combined?

Yes—triangulation across income, market, and asset methods is common; the key is coherent, evidence-backed weighting.

Report Types & Comparison

How different approaches stack up.

What’s the difference between a calculation valuation and a comprehensive valuation?

“Calculation” engagements are narrower and rely on limited procedures; “Comprehensive” engagements are deeper and include broader procedures and corroboration. Broader analysis of financials and qualitative factors usually improves decision-readiness.

Financing & Succession Planning

Using a valuation for capital and planning smooth transitions.

Can a valuation be used for financing or investors?

Yes—decision-makers look for clear methodology, credible assumptions, and fit-for-purpose reporting.

How does succession planning impact valuation?

Buy-ins, buyouts, and family transitions benefit from transparent, well-supported valuations to reduce disputes.
