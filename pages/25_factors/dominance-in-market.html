Dominance in the Market

When one has the experience of personally helping to execute a plan to have one's company dominate some segment of the market share; that person can recognize dominance when they see it. Without experience as a business owner operator a valuator could easily miss this factor completely or not know how to measure it—contrary to the Canada Income Tax Act’s requirement for “fair market value”.

What We Look For

  • Defensible market share: Sustained share leadership in a defined segment for 3–5+ years, not a one-off spike.
  • Pricing power: Ability to raise prices or hold margins despite competitors’ discounts.
  • Customer lock-in: Switching costs, integration depth, or ecosystem dependencies that keep churn structurally low.
  • Unit-economics advantage: Cost position or LTV/CAC that competitors struggle to match.
  • Speed/coverage edge: Distribution, supply chain, or service density competitors can’t replicate quickly.

Moats That Signal Dominance

  • Network effects: Product gets more valuable as more users/partners join.
  • Switching costs: Data migration, retraining, process re-engineering, or downstream risk.
  • Brand trust: Category shorthand; buyers default to you for perceived safety or quality.
  • Regulatory position: Licenses, certifications, or compliance track-record that’s costly to imitate.
  • Scale advantages: Preferred procurement, logistics rates, or exclusive access.
  • Distribution control: Exclusive channels/territories; locked-in resellers or key shelf space.

Evidence Checklist (bring to the valuation)

  • 3–5 years of market share data (by revenue/units) for your core segment(s).
  • Gross margin trend vs. nearest competitors; price realization vs. list.
  • Churn/retention cohorts; multi-year renewal rates; attach/upsell metrics.
  • Unit economics: LTV/CAC by cohort, payback period, contribution margin progression.
  • Distribution metrics: Fill rates, lead times, on-time performance, territory coverage.
  • Moat proof: Contracts (exclusivity), certifications, IP, integrations, ecosystem dependencies.

Practical Metrics & Thresholds

  • Share leadership: #1 or #2 share with stable or rising lead > 10 pts vs. #3.
  • Margin resilience: 200–500 bps margin premium vs. peer median during down cycles.
  • Retention: Net revenue retention ≥ 110% (recurring models) or repeat purchase rate > 60% (non-SaaS).
  • CAC payback: < 12 months (SMB) or < 24 months (enterprise/high ACV) with cohort consistency.
  • Pricing actions: Documented price increases without volume loss beyond temporary digestion.

How Dominance Impacts Valuation

  • Premium bands: Clear, defensible dominance typically supports a valuation premium (e.g., +10% to +35%) depending on moat strength and durability.
  • Cash-flow quality: More predictable renewal/retention reduces discount rate and risk adjustments.
  • Scenario skew: Upside scenarios gain probability (new geographies, adjacencies, pricing latitude).

Scorecard (1–5)

  • 5 — Category Controller: Multi-moat, durable share lead, consistent pricing power, superior unit economics.
  • 4 — Strong Leader: Clear leadership with 2+ active moats; mild vulnerability in one area.
  • 3 — Defended Niche: Defensible in a sub-segment; moats partial or emerging.
  • 2 — Parity Player: Undifferentiated vs. peers; share and margins vulnerable to promotions.
  • 1 — Fragmented/Tail: No leadership; price-taker; high churn; limited brand/distribution.

Red Flags That Reduce the Score

  • Leadership reliant on one account/channel or a short-term supply advantage.
  • Share/margins defended only by below-cost pricing or outsized rebates.
  • Retention anchored to punitive contract terms rather than product value.
  • Competitors rapidly closing gaps on service levels, integrations, or certifications.
